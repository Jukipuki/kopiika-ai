# Debt Management

## Overview

Debt is money you owe to a lender — it could be a credit card balance, a personal loan, or a mortgage. Not all debt is harmful: a mortgage builds equity in a property, and an education loan can increase future earning potential. However, high-interest consumer debt — particularly credit card balances — can grow quickly and limit your financial flexibility if left unchecked. In Ukraine, credit card interest rates of 35-55% APR are common, meaning unpaid balances can nearly double within two years if only minimum payments are made. Understanding how debt works, how interest accumulates, and having a clear repayment strategy helps you stay in control rather than letting balances dictate your monthly cash flow. The key distinction is between debt used as a tool with a plan for repayment and debt accumulated through untracked spending without a strategy to pay it down.

## Key Concepts

- **Good debt vs bad debt**: Some educators distinguish between debt that builds value (education, mortgage) and debt that finances consumption (credit card spending on non-essentials). The distinction is not absolute but helps with prioritization.
- **Interest rate (APR)**: The annual percentage rate you pay for borrowing. Ukrainian credit cards often charge 35-55% APR — meaning a ₴10,000 (~$250 USD) balance can generate ₴3,500-₴5,500 in interest per year.
- **Minimum payment trap**: Paying only the minimum keeps the account current but barely reduces the principal. On high-interest cards, most of the minimum payment goes to interest.
- **Avalanche method**: Paying off the highest-interest debt first while making minimums on everything else. Mathematically optimal — saves the most on interest.
- **Snowball method**: Paying off the smallest balance first for a psychological win, then rolling that payment into the next debt. Builds momentum and motivation.

## Practical Examples

### Example: Avalanche vs Snowball

A person has three debts:
- Credit card A: ₴15,000 (~$375 USD) at 48% APR
- Credit card B: ₴5,000 (~$125 USD) at 36% APR
- Personal loan: ₴30,000 (~$750 USD) at 22% APR

**Avalanche approach:** Attack the 48% card first (₴15,000). Despite being mid-size, it generates the most interest cost.
**Snowball approach:** Pay off the ₴5,000 card first for a quick win, then tackle the ₴15,000 card.

Both approaches are better than paying minimums across all three.

### Example: The True Cost of Minimum Payments

A ₴20,000 credit card balance at 45% APR with a 5% minimum payment (₴1,000/month initially) takes over 3 years to pay off and costs approximately ₴14,000 in interest — nearly 70% of the original balance.

## Actionable Takeaways

1. List all debts with their balances, interest rates, and minimum payments — this gives you the full picture. Many people carry debt across multiple sources without knowing the total amount owed or which balance is costing them the most in interest each month.
2. Choose a repayment strategy (avalanche for math efficiency, snowball for motivation) and commit to it. The avalanche method targets the highest-interest debt first, saving the most money over time. The snowball method targets the smallest balance first, providing quick wins that build momentum.
3. Avoid taking on new high-interest debt while repaying existing debt — this is the debt treadmill. Each new balance adds interest charges that compete with your repayment progress on existing obligations.
4. Consider whether consolidating high-interest debts into a lower-rate loan makes sense for your situation. Consolidation simplifies payments and can reduce total interest, but only if the new rate is genuinely lower and you avoid accumulating new debt on the freed-up credit lines.
5. Build a small emergency fund (₴10,000-₴15,000, roughly $250-$375 USD) alongside debt repayment to avoid going deeper into debt when surprises happen. Without a buffer, every unexpected expense becomes new debt.
