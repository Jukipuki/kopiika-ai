# The 50/30/20 Budget Rule

## Overview

The 50/30/20 rule is a simple budgeting framework that divides your after-tax income into three categories: 50% for needs, 30% for wants, and 20% for savings and debt repayment. Popularized by U.S. Senator Elizabeth Warren in her book *All Your Worth*, it provides a starting point for anyone who finds detailed line-by-line budgeting overwhelming. Instead of tracking dozens of expense categories, you only need to classify each expense into one of three buckets and check whether the totals stay within the target percentages. The exact percentages can be adjusted to fit individual circumstances — someone living in an expensive city may need 60% for needs, while someone with aggressive savings goals might allocate 30% or more to savings. The rule works best as a diagnostic tool: if your needs consume 70% of income, that is a signal worth investigating regardless of whether you adopt the framework permanently.

## Key Concepts

- **Needs (50%)**: Essential expenses you must pay — rent, utilities, groceries, basic transport, insurance, minimum debt payments. If you could not survive or maintain your job without it, it is a need.
- **Wants (30%)**: Non-essential spending that improves quality of life — dining out, entertainment, hobbies, upgrades, vacations. The line between need and want is personal: basic phone plan = need; premium plan = want.
- **Savings and debt repayment (20%)**: Money directed toward financial goals — emergency fund, extra debt payments beyond minimums, retirement savings, investment contributions.
- **After-tax income**: Your take-home pay after taxes and mandatory deductions. In Ukraine, this is your salary after PDFO (income tax) and YeSV (social contribution) if applicable.
- **Flexibility**: The 50/30/20 split is a guideline, not a law. In high cost-of-living cities, needs might take 60%. People with aggressive savings goals might target 30% savings.

## Practical Examples

### Example: Applying 50/30/20 to a Ukrainian Salary

Someone earning ₴30,000/month net (roughly $750 USD):
- **Needs (50% = ₴15,000)**: Rent ₴8,000, utilities ₴3,000, groceries ₴3,000, transport ₴1,000
- **Wants (30% = ₴9,000)**: Dining out ₴2,000, entertainment ₴2,500, subscriptions ₴1,500, shopping ₴3,000
- **Savings (20% = ₴6,000)**: Emergency fund ₴3,000, extra credit card payment ₴3,000

If their needs actually require ₴18,000 (60%), they might adjust to 60/20/20 — reducing wants to make room.

### Example: When 50/30/20 Does Not Fit

A person earning ₴20,000/month with ₴12,000 in fixed needs (60%) has only ₴8,000 left. Splitting that 30/20 means ₴6,000 wants and ₴4,000 savings — but ₴4,000/month in savings may not be enough. They might choose 60/15/25 to prioritize building an emergency fund faster.

## Actionable Takeaways

1. Calculate your after-tax monthly income — this is your 100% baseline. In Ukraine, this means your salary after ПДФО and ЄСВ deductions, plus any net freelance income. Knowing the exact number prevents budgeting against money you do not actually receive.
2. List all your needs and total them — if needs exceed 50%, that is not a failure but a signal to examine whether any "needs" are actually wants in disguise (premium phone plans, a car when public transit is viable) or whether your income needs attention.
3. Set a clear savings target (20% is a starting point) and automate the transfer on payday so the decision is made once rather than every month. Automatic transfers remove the temptation to skip saving.
4. Use the "wants" category as your flexible buffer — this is where you adjust first when income drops or an unexpected expense appears. Cutting wants temporarily is easier than restructuring needs.
5. Revisit your split every 3-6 months as income or circumstances change. A raise, a rent increase, or paying off a debt all shift the percentages and create an opportunity to reallocate.
