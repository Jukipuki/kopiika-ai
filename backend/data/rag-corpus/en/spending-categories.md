# Spending Categories

## Overview

Spending categories are labels assigned to your transactions — groceries, transport, entertainment, utilities, and so on. Categorizing your spending transforms a list of random charges into a clear picture of where your money goes, revealing priorities you may not have consciously chosen. When someone sees that 15% of their income goes to dining out, they can decide whether that aligns with their values or whether they would rather redirect some of that money elsewhere. Most banking apps like Monobank do this automatically using merchant category codes (MCC), which means the data is being collected whether you use it or not. Understanding what the categories mean, how they compare to each other, and how they change month to month helps you spot patterns, set realistic budgets, and make informed decisions about where to cut and where to maintain spending. Categories are the foundation of virtually every other personal finance concept — from the 50/30/20 rule to spending pattern analysis.

## Key Concepts

- **Category**: A group that describes the purpose of a transaction. Common categories include food, housing, transport, healthcare, entertainment, subscriptions, and shopping.
- **Fixed vs variable categories**: Fixed categories (rent, insurance) rarely change. Variable categories (dining out, shopping) fluctuate and offer the most room for adjustment.
- **Category percentages**: Looking at what percentage of your income goes to each category reveals your spending priorities — sometimes surprising ones.
- **Miscellaneous / uncategorized**: Transactions that do not fit standard categories. A high uncategorized amount usually means your tracking needs refinement.
- **Automated categorization**: Apps like Monobank auto-assign categories using merchant codes (MCC). These are usually accurate but sometimes need manual correction.

## Practical Examples

### Example: Category Breakdown Reveals a Pattern

Someone earning ₴35,000/month (roughly $875 USD) checks their monthly category breakdown:
- Housing (rent + utilities): ₴13,000 (37%)
- Food (groceries + dining): ₴7,500 (21%)
- Transport: ₴3,000 (9%)
- Entertainment: ₴3,500 (10%)
- Shopping: ₴4,000 (11%)
- Subscriptions: ₴2,000 (6%)
- Other: ₴2,000 (6%)

They notice food + entertainment together (31%) exceed housing (37%). This is neither good nor bad — but it is information they can act on if they want to shift priorities.

### Example: The "Latte Factor" in Categories

Small daily purchases (coffee, snacks, impulse buys) often hide inside larger categories. Someone spending ₴100/day on coffee and snacks (₴3,000/month, ~$75 USD) may not realize it until they see it broken out.

## Actionable Takeaways

1. Review your category breakdown monthly — most banking apps provide this automatically. The data is already there; the habit of actually looking at it once a month is what turns information into insight.
2. Pay attention to the percentage each category takes from your total income, not just the absolute amount. Knowing you spent ₴6,000 on dining out means less than knowing it represents 17% of your income — percentages make comparison meaningful.
3. Compare month-to-month to spot trends — a category growing 20% over three months signals a shift worth examining. Gradual increases often go unnoticed until they become significant, so regular comparison catches drift early.
4. Manually recategorize any transactions your bank app misclassifies for more accurate tracking. A grocery store that also sells household goods might categorize under "shopping" instead of "groceries," distorting your food spending picture.
5. Focus adjustment efforts on your largest variable categories — they offer the most room for change. Trying to save ₴200 on a ₴500 subscription is harder than finding ₴200 in a ₴7,000 food budget.
