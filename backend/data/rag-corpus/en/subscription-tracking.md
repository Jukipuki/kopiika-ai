# Subscription Tracking

## Overview

Subscriptions are recurring charges for services you use regularly — streaming platforms, software, gym memberships, cloud storage, and more. Because each individual charge is small and automatic, subscriptions can accumulate without notice until the combined monthly total becomes a significant budget item. A typical person might have 5-12 active subscriptions totaling ₴2,000-₴4,000 per month (roughly $50-$100 USD), and studies suggest that most people underestimate their subscription spending by 30-50%. The automatic nature of subscriptions is what makes them both convenient and dangerous: you sign up once, and the charges continue indefinitely whether you use the service or not. Free trials that convert to paid plans, forgotten annual renewals, and services replaced by alternatives all contribute to what financial educators call "subscription creep." Periodically auditing your subscriptions — reviewing each one and asking whether it still provides enough value to justify the cost — helps you pay only for what you actually use and reclaim money that might be better directed elsewhere.

## Key Concepts

- **Subscription creep**: The gradual accumulation of subscriptions over time, often without conscious decisions. A free trial converts to paid, a new app adds a monthly fee, and suddenly you have 8-12 active subscriptions.
- **Phantom subscriptions**: Services you pay for but no longer use — a language app you opened twice, a music service you replaced with another, or a second cloud storage plan.
- **Annual vs monthly billing**: Many services offer a discount for annual payment (often 15-30% off). This saves money but makes it harder to notice the charge and easier to forget about.
- **Subscription audit**: A periodic review (quarterly or semi-annually) of all recurring charges. The goal is to cancel, downgrade, or pause services that do not provide enough value.

## Practical Examples

### Example: The Monthly Subscription Stack

A person reviews their bank statement and finds:
- Netflix: ₴399/month
- Spotify: ₴149/month
- YouTube Premium: ₴199/month
- Cloud storage: ₴79/month
- Gym membership: ₴1,200/month
- Language app: ₴249/month
- VPN: ₴99/month

**Total: ₴2,374/month (₴28,488/year)** — roughly $60 USD/month. They realize they have not used the language app in 3 months and watch YouTube rarely. Canceling those two saves ₴448/month (₴5,376/year).

### Example: Free Trial Trap

Someone signs up for a 7-day free trial of a productivity app. They forget to cancel. Six months later, they discover ₴1,494 in charges (₴249/month x 6) for an app they used once.

## Actionable Takeaways

1. Review your bank or card statement for all recurring charges — Monobank's category view makes this straightforward by grouping subscription-like payments together. List every recurring charge with its amount and frequency to see the true total.
2. For each subscription, ask two questions: "Did I use this in the last 30 days?" and "Would I sign up for it again today at this price?" If the answer to either is no, that subscription is a candidate for cancellation or downgrade.
3. Services that are no longer used can typically be reactivated later if needed, so canceling is rarely permanent. The fear of losing access to something unused costs real money every month for zero benefit.
4. Set a calendar reminder 1-2 weeks before annual subscription renewals so you can make a deliberate decision before the charge appears. Annual subscriptions are easy to forget, and the charge often goes unnoticed for months.
5. Consider sharing family plans where available — splitting a ₴399/month streaming service across 3-4 household members cuts per-person costs to ₴100-₴130/month. Many services (Netflix, Spotify, YouTube Premium) offer family or shared plans at significant discounts.
