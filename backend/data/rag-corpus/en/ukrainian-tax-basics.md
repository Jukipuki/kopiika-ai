# Ukrainian Tax Basics

## Overview

Understanding the basics of Ukraine's tax system helps with financial planning and avoiding surprises — particularly the difference between gross (pre-tax) and net (take-home) income, which is the real number available for your budget. The Ukrainian tax system has several key taxes for individuals: PDFO (personal income tax at 18%), military levy (5% as of 2025-2026), YeSV (unified social contribution, paid by employers), and the FOP (sole proprietor) system for freelancers and entrepreneurs. For regular employees, these taxes are withheld automatically from salary, meaning a ₴40,000 gross salary results in approximately ₴30,800 take-home pay (roughly $770 USD) — a 23% reduction that catches some people off guard when they see their first paycheck. For freelancers and FOP holders, understanding which tax group to register under and when payments are due is essential for avoiding penalties and maintaining accurate cash flow planning. This document is an educational overview of how these taxes work, not tax advice for individual situations.

## Key Concepts

- **PDFO (personal income tax)**: The main income tax — 18% on wages and most other income. For employees, it is withheld automatically by the employer.
- **Military levy**: An additional 5% levy (as of 2025-2026) on income, withheld alongside PDFO. This means the combined withholding rate for employees is 23%.
- **YeSV (unified social contribution)**: A social security contribution — 22% of salary, paid by the employer. For FOPs (sole proprietors), it is a fixed amount or percentage depending on the tax group.
- **FOP (sole proprietor)**: A business structure for freelancers and small businesses. There are several groups with different taxation: Group 1 (small retail, ~₴303/month fixed), Group 2 (services, ~₴1,514/month fixed), Group 3 (5% of revenue, or 3% with VAT).
- **Tax social benefit**: A reduction in the tax base for low-income employees. Applied automatically if salary does not exceed a defined threshold.

## Practical Examples

### Example: Net Salary Calculation for an Employee

An employer sets a gross salary of ₴40,000 (~$1,000 USD):
- PDFO (18%): ₴7,200
- Military levy (5%): ₴2,000
- **Net (take-home): ₴30,800**

Additionally, the employer pays YeSV (22%): ₴8,800. Total cost to the company: ₴48,800.

### Example: FOP Group 3 vs Employment Comparison

A freelancer earning ₴50,000/month (~$1,250 USD) as FOP Group 3 (5%):
- Unified tax (5%): ₴2,500
- YeSV (minimum): ~₴1,760
- **Net income: ~₴45,740**

As an employee with the same ₴50,000 gross:
- PDFO + military levy (23%): ₴11,500
- **Take-home: ₴38,500**

The difference is significant, but FOP status does not include paid vacation, sick leave, or other employment benefits.

## Actionable Takeaways

1. Understanding the difference between gross (before tax) and net (take-home) salary matters because net is the real number available for budgeting. When evaluating job offers, knowing that a ₴40,000 gross offer means only ₴30,800 in your account after PDFO and military levy prevents budgeting surprises.
2. If you are a FOP, set aside tax money immediately when income arrives so quarterly tax payments are not a surprise. A separate "tax jar" in Monobank or a dedicated account prevents accidentally spending money that belongs to the tax authority.
3. Check whether you qualify for the tax social benefit (податкова соціальна пільга) — it can reduce your PDFO amount for low-income earners. The threshold and benefit amount are updated annually.
4. Keep records of income and expenses — they may be needed for tax declarations, especially for FOP holders. Good records also help you verify that employer withholdings are correct and support any claims for deductions.
5. When considering a switch between employment and FOP status, calculate the full difference including lost social benefits (paid vacation, sick leave, maternity leave, employer YeSV contributions). The take-home pay difference can be significant, but so is the loss of employment protections.
