# Interest and Credit

## Overview

Interest is the cost of borrowing money — or the reward for lending it. When you take a loan or carry a credit card balance, you pay interest to the lender. When you deposit money in a bank, you earn interest because the bank is effectively borrowing from you. This dual nature makes interest one of the most important concepts in personal finance: it can work powerfully for you (when saving or investing) or against you (when carrying debt). In Ukraine, this contrast is especially stark — credit card APRs of 35-55% can rapidly multiply balances, while bank deposit rates of 10-16% offer meaningful returns on savings. Understanding how interest is calculated, the difference between simple and compound interest, and how credit products like grace periods work helps you make informed decisions about borrowing and saving. Many costly financial mistakes stem from a misunderstanding of how interest accumulates over time, particularly on revolving credit card debt.

## Key Concepts

- **APR (Annual Percentage Rate)**: The yearly interest rate charged on borrowed money. Ukrainian credit cards typically charge 35-55% APR — among the highest consumer credit rates globally.
- **APY (Annual Percentage Yield)**: The yearly return on deposited money, including compound interest. A 12% APY deposit compounds to more than a flat 12% over the year.
- **Simple vs compound interest**: Simple interest is calculated only on the original amount. Compound interest is calculated on the original amount plus accumulated interest — this makes debt grow faster and savings grow faster too.
- **Minimum payment**: The smallest amount you can pay on a credit card to keep the account current. Paying only the minimum mostly covers interest, barely reducing the principal balance.
- **Grace period**: The time between a purchase and when interest starts accruing (typically 25-55 days for credit cards). Paying the full statement balance within the grace period means paying zero interest.

## Practical Examples

### Example: The True Cost of a Credit Card Purchase

You buy a ₴10,000 item (~$250 USD) on a credit card at 48% APR and pay only the minimum (5% of balance, decreasing):
- After 1 year: you have paid ₴5,800 but still owe ₴7,400 (interest keeps adding)
- Total cost to pay off: approximately ₴16,500 over 3+ years
- You paid ₴6,500 in interest — 65% of the purchase price

If you had paid the full balance within the grace period: ₴0 in interest.

### Example: Deposit Interest Working For You

₴50,000 in a bank deposit at 14% APY compounded monthly:
- After 1 year: ₴57,490 (you earned ₴7,490 — more than a flat 14% = ₴7,000 due to compounding)
- After 3 years: ₴75,700
- After 5 years: ₴99,700 — nearly doubling your money

## Actionable Takeaways

1. Checking the APR before using a credit card for purchases that cannot be paid off within the grace period helps avoid unexpected interest charges — at Ukrainian rates of 35-55%, even a small balance grows quickly.
2. Paying the full credit card statement balance each month avoids interest entirely — many financial educators consider this the single most impactful credit habit a person can build.
3. Understanding the difference between the grace period and minimum payment clarifies two very different outcomes — one avoids interest costs entirely, the other mostly pays interest while barely reducing the principal.
4. When comparing savings products, looking at APY (not just the advertised rate) reveals actual returns including compounding — the difference between advertised rate and APY grows with higher rates and more frequent compounding.
5. Calculating the total cost of credit before borrowing reveals the real price of a purchase — the sticker price of an item is not its true cost when financed at high interest over months or years.
