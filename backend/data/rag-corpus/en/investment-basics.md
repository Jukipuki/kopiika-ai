# Investment Basics

## Overview

Investing means putting money into assets — stocks, bonds, funds, or real estate — with the expectation that they will grow in value over time. Unlike saving, which preserves your money with minimal risk, investing involves the possibility that your money can grow significantly faster than in a bank account but can also lose value temporarily or permanently. The fundamental trade-off is between risk and potential return: safer investments like government bonds offer modest but predictable growth, while riskier assets like stocks can deliver higher returns over long periods but experience significant short-term swings. For most people, the question is not whether to invest but when and how — and the answer usually depends on having a stable financial foundation first (emergency fund, no high-interest debt). This document covers foundational investment concepts only and does not recommend specific investments or strategies. Understanding these basics prepares you to evaluate options and ask informed questions when you are ready to begin.

## Key Concepts

- **Saving vs investing**: Saving preserves money (low risk, low return — bank deposits). Investing aims to grow money (higher risk, potentially higher return). Most financial educators suggest having an emergency fund in savings before beginning to invest.
- **Risk and return**: Generally, higher potential returns come with higher risk. Government bonds are lower risk than stocks, but historically offer lower long-term returns.
- **Diversification**: Spreading money across different types of investments to reduce the impact of any single one performing poorly. The phrase "do not put all your eggs in one basket" captures this idea.
- **Compound growth**: When investment returns generate their own returns over time. A 10% annual return on ₴100,000 (roughly $2,500 USD) produces ₴10,000 in year one, but ₴11,000 in year two (10% of ₴110,000), and so on.
- **Time horizon**: How long you plan to keep money invested. Longer horizons (10+ years) can tolerate more short-term volatility because there is time to recover from dips.

## Practical Examples

### Example: Saving vs Investing Over 10 Years

Someone sets aside ₴5,000/month (~$125 USD):
- **Bank deposit at 10% annual**: After 10 years, roughly ₴1,000,000
- **Diversified investment at hypothetical 15% annual**: After 10 years, roughly ₴1,400,000

The investment scenario has higher potential return but also carries risk — in a bad year, the portfolio could lose 20-30% of its value temporarily. The bank deposit is predictable but may not keep pace with inflation.

### Example: Why Starting Early Matters

Two people each invest ₴3,000/month at a hypothetical 12% annual return. Person A starts at 25, Person B starts at 35. By age 55, Person A has accumulated significantly more — not because they invested more per month, but because compound growth had 10 extra years to work.

## Actionable Takeaways

1. Understand the difference between saving (capital preservation in bank deposits) and investing (capital growth with risk in assets like stocks or bonds) before committing money. They serve different purposes and mixing them up can lead to either missed growth opportunities or unexpected losses.
2. Consider building a 3-6 month emergency fund in accessible savings before allocating money to investments. Investing money you might need soon forces you to sell at potentially bad times, turning temporary market dips into real losses.
3. Learn about different asset types (stocks, bonds, index funds, government securities like Ukrainian ОВДП) before choosing where to invest. Each has different risk levels, time horizons, and minimum amounts, and understanding these differences prevents uninformed decisions.
4. Consider your time horizon — money you need within 1-2 years is generally better kept in savings because short-term market volatility could reduce its value right when you need it. Investments tend to reward patience over longer periods.
5. Recognize that all investments carry risk, and past performance does not guarantee future results. Skepticism toward "guaranteed returns" promises is healthy — legitimate investments always disclose risk.
