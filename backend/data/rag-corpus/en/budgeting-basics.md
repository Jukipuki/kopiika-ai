# Budgeting Basics

## Overview

A budget is a plan for how you spend and save your money each month. At its core, budgeting means writing down your income, listing your expenses, and making sure the numbers work before the month begins — rather than wondering where everything went after the fact. It helps you see where your money goes, make intentional choices, and avoid running out of funds before your next paycheck. Budgeting does not mean restricting yourself — it means giving every dollar (or hryvnia) a purpose. Many people avoid budgeting because they associate it with deprivation, but in practice a budget gives you permission to spend freely within the categories you have chosen. Whether you use a spreadsheet, a banking app like Monobank's built-in analytics, or pen and paper, the mechanism matters less than the habit of regularly comparing what you planned to spend with what you actually spent.

## Key Concepts

- **Income**: The total money you receive regularly — salary, freelance payments, side income, government assistance. Your budget starts here because you cannot plan spending without knowing how much comes in. If your income varies month to month, many educators suggest budgeting based on your lowest recent month as a conservative baseline.
- **Fixed expenses**: Costs that stay roughly the same each month — rent, loan payments, insurance premiums, subscriptions. These are predictable and form the foundation of any budget because they must be paid regardless.
- **Variable expenses**: Costs that change month to month — groceries, dining out, entertainment, transportation fuel. These are where most budgeting flexibility exists and where small adjustments can make the biggest difference.
- **Budget surplus**: When your income exceeds your spending. This positive gap can be directed toward savings, debt repayment, or building an emergency fund — it represents progress toward financial stability.
- **Budget deficit**: When your spending exceeds your income — a signal to review and adjust your plan before the shortfall becomes a recurring pattern that leads to debt accumulation.

## Practical Examples

### Example: Monthly Budget for a Young Professional

Imagine someone earning ~₴30,000/month (roughly $750 USD at current rates) in Kyiv. Their budget might look like:
- Rent: ₴10,000
- Utilities: ₴3,000
- Groceries: ₴4,000
- Transport: ₴1,500
- Entertainment: ₴2,000
- Savings: ₴5,000
- Subscriptions & misc: ₴4,500

By writing this down, they can see that entertainment and miscellaneous spending is their largest flexible category — and decide if that aligns with their priorities.

### Example: Tracking a Budget Gap

A freelancer notices they earned ₴25,000 but spent ₴28,000. By reviewing categories, they discover ₴4,500 went to unplanned shopping. Next month, they set a ₴2,000 shopping limit and redirect the difference to their emergency fund.

## Actionable Takeaways

1. List all your income sources and total them — this is your starting number. Include salary, freelance income, and any recurring transfers. If your income varies, use the average of the last three months as your planning figure.
2. Track every expense for one full month to see where money actually goes. Banking apps like Monobank categorize transactions automatically, which removes the burden of manual logging. The first month of tracking often reveals surprises.
3. Group expenses into fixed and variable categories to identify where you have flexibility. Fixed costs are harder to change quickly, but variable costs can often be adjusted within the same month.
4. Compare total spending to total income — the gap (positive or negative) shows your financial direction. A positive gap means you have room to save or repay debt; a negative gap means adjustments are needed before the pattern compounds.
5. Review and adjust your budget monthly — it is a living document, not a one-time exercise. Life changes (a raise, a new expense, a seasonal cost like heating) mean last month's plan may not fit this month.
